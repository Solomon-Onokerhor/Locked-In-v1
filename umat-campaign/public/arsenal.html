AI Engineering
Prompts.
Copy-paste these into ChatGPT or Gemini. Each one is calibrated for the exact way UMaT engineering and science exams are structured.
🧠
The Concept Breakdown Prompt
Use when: Studying a new topic for the first time
"Act as an MIT professor teaching UMaT students. Explain [INSERT TOPIC, e.g. Thermodynamics / Signal Processing / Structural Analysis] using simple real-world analogies. List the core formulas, explain when to use each, and give me the 3 most common exam mistakes students make on this topic."
💡 Pro tip: Replace [INSERT TOPIC] with your exact lecture title. The more specific you are, the better the output.
💻
The Code Debugger Prompt
Use when: Your lab code is throwing errors
"I wrote this [C++/Python/MATLAB] code for my lab and it throws [PASTE ERROR]. Do NOT just fix it — point out the exact line where the logic breaks and explain WHY it fails, so I actually understand it. Then show the corrected version with comments on every change."
💡 Pro tip: Paste your full code block after the prompt. The "explain WHY" instruction stops AI from just patching without teaching.
📝
The Past Questions Analyst
Use when: Preparing for exams using past papers
"Here is a past exam question from my [COURSE NAME] paper: [PASTE QUESTION]. Walk me through the full solution step by step, showing every formula substitution. Then explain the underlying concept so I can solve any similar question independently."
💡 Pro tip: After getting the answer, ask: "Give me 3 variations of this question at increasing difficulty."
🗓️
The Study Plan Generator
Use when: Exams are approaching and you're behind
"I have [X] days before my [SUBJECT] exam. My weak areas are [LIST THEM]. My strongest area is [LIST IT]. Build me a day-by-day study schedule that prioritises my weakest topics first, includes 30-minute daily review sessions for strong topics, and builds in rest days so I don't burn out."
💡 Pro tip: Be honest about your weak areas. The schedule only works if it's calibrated to your actual gaps.
Section 02
The Locked-In
Focus Protocol.
A daily system used by high-performing UMaT students to study harder, retain more, and avoid the crash that comes from grinding without a system.
1

The 50/10 Deep Work Rule

Study in 50-minute locked-in blocks followed by a strict 10-minute rest. During the 50 minutes: phone in another room, one tab open, one task only. During the 10 minutes: stand up, walk, drink water — no social media. This single habit doubles retention compared to marathon unfocused sessions.

2

Environment Design — Turn Your Phone Into a Dumb Phone

Before each study block: set your phone to Airplane Mode, place it face-down outside the room. Use a physical timer (or the Locked-In platform timer) instead. Notifications are engineered to break focus — remove the option entirely. Your room should have one thing visible: your notes.

3

The Night-Before Ritual (5 Minutes)

Every night before sleep, write down the single most important topic you need to master tomorrow. Just one. Lay your notes out. Set a start time alarm — not a "wake up" alarm. This small ritual eliminates morning decision fatigue and cuts 2 hours of wasted time per week.

4

Active Recall — Never Re-Read Passively

After every 50-minute block, close your notes and spend 5 minutes writing down everything you remember. This is called active recall — it forces your brain to retrieve information under conditions similar to an exam. Students who use active recall score 50% higher than those who re-read the same notes repeatedly.

5

The Weekly Review (Sunday, 20 Minutes)

Every Sunday, review the week: which topics did you cover? What still feels shaky? Plan your 3 most important study blocks for the coming week. Students who do weekly reviews are 3x less likely to be caught off-guard by exams.

⚡ The Locked-In Streak System

  • Track your focus blocks daily on the Locked-In platform — each day you complete 3+ blocks builds your streak
  • Streaks create identity: you stop being someone trying to study, and start being someone who studies
  • Share your streak on the Locked-In community board to create public accountability
  • Miss a day? The protocol is simple: restart. No guilt, no drama. Just restart.

Section 04
The Calculus
Survival Kit.
Calculus kills more engineering students than any other course. These prompts are built to explain it clearly, step by step, the way your lecturer sometimes can't.
📉
The Differentiation Explainer
Use when: You don't understand why or how to differentiate
"I'm a UMaT engineering student struggling with differentiation. Explain the concept of a derivative from scratch using a real-world speed/distance example. Then show me step-by-step how to differentiate [INSERT FUNCTION e.g. f(x) = 3x³ + 2x² - 5x + 1] using the power rule, product rule, and chain rule where applicable. Highlight which rule applies at each step."
💡 Pro tip: Paste the exact function from your assignment or past question for a worked solution you can study.
∫
The Integration Solver
Use when: You're stuck on an integration problem
"Solve this integral for me: [PASTE INTEGRAL]. Show every single step — do not skip any algebra. After solving it, tell me which integration technique you used (substitution, by parts, partial fractions, etc.) and explain in one sentence WHY that technique was the right choice for this type of problem."
💡 Pro tip: After solving, ask "Give me 3 similar integrals that use the same technique so I can practise." This builds pattern recognition fast.
📈
The Limits & L'Hôpital Prompt
Use when: You get 0/0 or ∞/∞ and don't know what to do
"Evaluate this limit: [PASTE LIMIT PROBLEM]. First check if direct substitution works. If it gives an indeterminate form (0/0 or ∞/∞), apply L'Hôpital's Rule and explain each step. If L'Hôpital doesn't apply, show the algebraic manipulation needed. End with a one-line explanation of what this limit represents physically."
💡 Pro tip: Understanding what a limit "means physically" (e.g. the speed of an object at an instant) locks in the concept far better than just memorising the rule.
〰️
The Differential Equations Breakdown
Use when: You have a DE and have no idea where to start
"I have this differential equation: [PASTE DE]. First, classify it — is it ordinary or partial? What is its order and degree? Is it linear or non-linear? Then solve it step-by-step showing all working. If there are initial conditions, apply them to find the particular solution. Finally, tell me one real engineering scenario where this type of DE appears."
💡 Pro tip: The "classify it first" instruction forces the AI to identify the correct solving method before jumping in — this mirrors how your exam expects you to approach DEs.
